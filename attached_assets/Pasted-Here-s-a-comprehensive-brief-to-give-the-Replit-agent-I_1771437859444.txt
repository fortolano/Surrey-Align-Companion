Here's a comprehensive brief to give the Replit agent. It explains the governance model, entity-scoped data, and how everything
  connects — from the perspective of someone building a mobile app that consumes this API:

  ---
  SurreyAlign Data Model — How Goals & Leadership Work (For the Mobile App Developer)

  Critical Concept: Everything is Entity-Scoped, NOT User-Scoped

  SurreyAlign does NOT work like a personal todo app where each user has their own goals. Goals, boards, progress indicators, and actions
   all belong to entities — organizations, councils, committees, and wards. Users access them based on their leadership roles within
  those entities.

  Think of it like a company: the "Q1 Revenue Target" doesn't belong to John in Sales — it belongs to the Sales Department. John can see
  it because he works in Sales. If John transfers to Marketing, he loses access to the Sales goal and gains access to Marketing's goals.
  Same principle here.

  ---
  The Entity Types

  | Entity Type  | What It Is                                                   | Examples
          | DB Table                  |
  |--------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------
  --------|---------------------------|
  | Stake        | The top-level geographic unit (like a regional district)     | Surrey British Columbia Stake
          | stakes                    |
  | Ward/Branch  | A local congregation within the stake (like a branch office) | Surrey 1st, Brookswood, White Rock, Richmond 2nd
          | wards                     |
  | Organization | A functional group within a ward or at stake level           | Surrey 4th Relief Society, Stake Primary, Brookswood
  Young Men | organizations             |
  | Council      | A governance body that reviews and approves goals            | Stake Council, Surrey 1st Ward Council, High Council
          | councils (kind=council)   |
  | Committee    | A cross-cutting working group under a council                | Stake Missionary Committee, Youth Leadership Committee
          | councils (kind=committee) |

  Key structure:
  - 1 Stake → 8 Wards
  - Each ward has ~7 organizations (Bishopric, Elders Quorum, Relief Society, Primary, Sunday School, Young Men, Young Women)
  - The stake has 6 stake-level organizations (Stake Presidency, Stake RS, Stake Primary, Stake YM, Stake YW, Stake SS)
  - Total: ~64 ward orgs + 6 stake orgs = 70 organizations
  - 13 councils + 10 committees = 23 councils/committees

  ---
  How Goals Work (Entity-Owned, Not User-Owned)

  A goal is created at one of four scopes:

  | Scope        | Who Creates It       | Who Approves It     | Example                                               |
  |--------------|----------------------|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
  | stake        | Stake Presidency     | Stake Council votes | "Strengthen temple worship across the stake"          |
  | ward         | Bishop (ward leader) | Ward Council votes  | "Increase ministering visits in our ward"             |
  | organization | Org President        | Auto-approved       | "Launch temple prep class" (created by RS Presidency) |
  | committee    | Committee Chair      | Auto-approved       | "Coordinate stake youth conference"                   |

  Goals cascade DOWN the hierarchy:

  STAKE GOAL (created by Stake Presidency)
    ↓ approved by Stake Council
    ↓ released to wards (manual step)
    ↓
    ├── Ward Council sees it → can create Progress Indicators on their board
    ├── Ward organizations see it → can create Progress Indicators on their boards
    ├── Stake organizations see it → can create PIs on their boards
    └── Committees see it → can create PIs on their boards

  A single stake goal may be visible to 95 different entities across the stake. Each entity responds to the goal independently by
  creating their own Progress Indicators.

  ---
  How Users See Goals (Role-Based Access, Not Ownership)

  Users don't "own" goals. They see goals based on which entities they belong to through their callings (church leadership positions):

  | User Role                                                           | What Entities They Belong To
                                                     | What Goals They See                                                  |
  |---------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------
  ---------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
  | Stake President                                                     | All entities
                                                     | ALL goals in the stake                                               |
  | High Councilor (e.g., assigned to Surrey 4th + Primary stewardship) | Surrey 4th ward (all 7 orgs), Stake Primary, all ward Primary
  orgs, High Council, Stake Council, their committees | Goals assigned to ANY of those entities                              |
  | Bishop (e.g., Surrey 1st)                                           | Surrey 1st ward (all 7 orgs), Surrey 1st Ward Council
                                                     | Stake goals released to Surrey 1st + ward-level goals for Surrey 1st |
  | RS President (e.g., Surrey 4th RS)                                  | Surrey 4th Relief Society, possibly Surrey 4th Ward Council
                                                     | Goals assigned to her organization                                   |

  The API handles all of this automatically. When you call GET /goals, the backend resolves which entities the authenticated user belongs
   to and returns only the goals they should see. You don't need to pass entity IDs or role information — the bearer token identifies the
   user and the server figures out access.

  ---
  Progress Indicators (PIs) — The Execution Layer

  When an entity (org, council, committee) receives a goal, leaders on that entity's execution board create Progress Indicators —
  observable commitments that show the goal is being worked on.

  PIs belong to entities, not users. The "Surrey 4th Relief Society" board might have a PI called "Families with temple plans" for a
  stake goal about temple worship. That PI belongs to the organization, not to the sister who created it.

  There are 3 types of Progress Indicators:

  | Type                | What It Tracks                          | Example                                                |
  |---------------------|-----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
  | Achieve (milestone) | Binary — done or not done               | "Temple prep class launched" (yes/no)                  |
  | Growth (increase)   | Numeric increase from start to target   | "Families with temple plans: 0 → 20" (currently at 12) |
  | Momentum            | Subjective assessment on a 0-100% scale | "Ministering quality" (leader rates 0/25/50/75/100)    |

  Each PI can have up to 3 per entity per goal. Each PI has Actions — concrete tasks that drive the PI forward.

  ---
  Actions — What People Actually Do

  Actions are the specific tasks attached to a Progress Indicator:
  - "Coordinate temple trip for March 15" (progress: 75%)
  - "Run temple prep class for new converts" (progress: 100%, completed)
  - "Follow up with 5 families about temple recommends" (progress: 50%)

  When all actions reach 100% AND the PI metric target is met, the PI auto-completes.

  ---
  How the Execution Endpoint Works

  GET /goals/{id}/execution returns the PIs and Actions for a goal, grouped by entity:

  Goal: "Strengthen temple worship"
    │
    ├── Entity: Surrey 4th Relief Society
    │     ├── PI: "Families with temple plans" (Growth, 60%)
    │     │     ├── Action: "Coordinate temple trips" (75%)
    │     │     └── Action: "Temple prep class" (100%)
    │     └── PI: "RS sisters with recommend" (Momentum, 50%)
    │
    ├── Entity: Surrey 4th Ward Council
    │     └── PI: "Ward temple night monthly" (Achieve, not started)
    │           └── Action: "Schedule first temple night" (0%)
    │
    └── Entity: Stake Primary
          └── PI: "Children learning about temples" (Momentum, 75%)

  The API only returns entities the authenticated user can see. A ward RS President would see only the "Surrey 4th Relief Society"
  entity. A High Councilor with Primary stewardship would see "Surrey 4th Relief Society" + "Surrey 4th Ward Council" + "Stake Primary" +
   all ward Primary orgs.

  ---
  What This Means for Mobile App Development

  1. Don't build "My Goals" as a personal list. Build it as "Goals I can see based on my calling." The API already filters correctly.
  2. Group goals by scope in the UI. The scope field tells you: stake goals are the big picture, ward goals are local,
  organization/committee goals are team-specific.
  3. The execution view is entity-grouped. When showing PIs for a goal, display them under entity headers ("Surrey 4th Relief Society",
  "Ward Council", etc.) — not as a flat list.
  4. Progress is hierarchical: Goal progress = average of its PIs. PI progress = metric calculation + action completion. Show progress at
   each level.
  5. period_id matters. Goals belong to planning periods (cycles). The API defaults to the current cycle. If no goals appear, the user
  may need to select a different period. The current active period is period_id=7 (Annual 2026).
  6. Auto-summary mode: If the execution endpoint returns meta.includes_actions: false, it means there were too many entities (>15) and
  the response was automatically summarized. Show the summary view and let users tap into a specific entity to get full detail using
  ?entity_type=organization&entity_id=17.

  ---
  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  The fundamental mental model shift: Most app developers assume data is user-centric — "show me MY stuff." Church leadership is
  entity-centric — "show me what my ORGANIZATION is doing." A user doesn't have goals; their organization has goals. A user doesn't have
  progress indicators; their entity's board has progress indicators. The user is a window into entity-scoped data, not the owner of it.
  Once the Replit agent grasps this, everything else falls into place — the API responses make sense, the grouping makes sense, and the
  access model makes sense.
